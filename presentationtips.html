9  Presentation Tips

Your presentations in the industry project are a form of public speaking that we often dread. Unfortunately, there is no getaway from this, and you must conquer your fear.

The skills for oral presentation vary from that of written report. In your presentation, you should try to use laymen’s terms so that your audience, who may be unfamiliar with the field of your study, understands what your are trying to say.

Don’t use the words with heavy terminologies, if required then break it down and explain what they mean before you use them.

Motivate your business problems with familiar scenarios. Instead of text try to use visual aids (such as pictures, video, tables etc.) to get your ideas out.

Towards the end of your oral presentation summarize your key findings with managerial implications.

9.1 Mandatory Rules

Plan your presentation to leave sufficient time for questions and comments. This is compulsory for mid-term presentation. This means that presentations should have no more than 5-7 slides. You can include more slides if there are lot of figures. For final project presentation, leave time for general comment from the client.

Use no more than 2 slides for the introduction (e.g., describing business problem). It is critical to get to your actual business solutions as soon as possible.

No more than 1 slide, and no more than 3 minutes, on background information. Of course, you should be familiar with the related business information in order to answer questions about it.

Only put material on slides that you are actually going to talk about.

When describing results from data analysis, focus on business rather than on model or statistics.

Each project group member is expected to participate in presentations.

9.2 About Slides

Font and Layout: Use a huge font and a simple slide layout. Remove any information or visual element that is not strictly necessary for understanding the slide. Don’t change the font size to fit the text.

Minimize text: the fewer bullets and the fewer words per slide the better. No full sentences on slides. The slides should support your talk, not replace it.

Title: Use informative titles for your slides. Ideally, your main message should be decipherable by simply reading the titles of your slides in succession.

Never use math symbols that have not been introduced.

Simplicity: Better to make one point well on a slide than to overwhelm an audience with text.

Roadmap: Use a roadmap if you would like to outline your presentation. It can also be helpful to revisit during the presentation to tell the audience where you are in the talk.

Color: The traditional red-blue colors are bad for those who are color blind.

Slide Tone: If possible, play a subtle sound when slide is advanced to the next slide. This enables audience to hear an auditory signal (from a low C to a high C note) of their progress through the slides, which helps blind people follow the presentation.

Background Color: For presentations in large auditoriums, go black background with white text.

Graphs: Be consistent in using fonts for graphs. It is advised to keep graphs in central location on slides.

Animations: Avoid too many animations in your presentations.

9.3 About Content

Introduction: It is an important slide to tell audience what you are going to talk about. At the same time, it is a trap where presenters loose a lot of time. Be careful.

Motivations: Do not use quotes for motivation. Use real business examples to motivate your talk.

Structure: Talk into clearly identified segments, and ensure your audience knows where you are.

Tables/Figures/Graphs: Don’t just throw these visual elements on the slides. Talk about them. For example, when presenting a table, first say why we need to look at that table. Then, describe the table layout (e.g., what rows and columns are about), explain the units in which variables are measured, and focus on the cells (e.g., individual numbers).

9.4 General Tips

Stand up, stand next to the screen, and look at your audience. Use your fingers to point. No laser pointers.

Dress in business casuals for the presentations. Don’t present in shorts, t-shirts, and thongs.

Make a conscious decision on which sections need more talking and which can be skimmed.

There is no penalty for finishing early.

The easiest way to prepare a presentation is to imagine you are having a dinner table discussion with your family on the topic. The goal of your presentation is to ensure your family members understand you by the time dinner is over.

Transitions are key. Tell the audience where you are going. Say when one subject is done and you are moving to the next subject. Say things like: “I have described the business problems. Now we are going to …” Use back references. For example, say “I told you that social media marketing is going to be crucial for the client to connect with its customers. The way I plan to implement the client’s social media marketing is …”

Mention the limitations of your report.

You won’t be able to avoid a situation where somebody asks you a question disagreeing with your solutions. In answering questions of this type, use the principle “first the bad news, then the good news.” You first admit that your solutions have limitations. Do this without hesitation, because your audience needs to understand that you are a reasonable person and are aware of where your solutions fail. So, say something like: “yes, I agree with you that these limitations may not help realizing the target ROI for the client.” After you are on the same page with the audience, you can now say something positive about your solution, like “But I still think that given the business environment that the client is facing, social media marketing will provide optimal returns in comparision to other touchpoints.”
