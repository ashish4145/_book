8  Writing Tips

Man has an instinctive tendency to speak, as we see in the babble of our young children, but no child has an instinctive tendency to bake, brew, or write.
Charles Darwin (1871), The Descent of Man

Unlike speaking, where one has a conversational partner, writing is an unnatural act as one has to imagine her audience. So at first, it may seem daunting to write the report.

Putting ideas into words is a severe test as writing about something, even something you know well, usually shows you that you didn’t know it as well as you thought.

The first words you choose are usually wrong; you have to rewrite sentences over and over to get them exactly right. And your ideas won’t just be imprecise, but incomplete too. Half the ideas that end up in your report will be ones you thought of while you were writing it.

Writing down your ideas always makes you more precise and more complete. Those who haven’t written about a topic have no fully formed ideas about it.

You can follow the Snowflake method of writing where you begin with simplistic deep theme and then, over time, develop and adds complexity. In your project, you can just make a list of bullets points on your various ideas, solutions, and strategies to solve the business problem. And then, during the execution period, you further develop those ideas and add complexity, converting your bullet points into a well structured report.

8.1 Report Writing

Your report is a professional document that has a formal structure. Your report communicates the results of an industry project. The formal structure of a report is determined based on answers to these two questions: 1) who is the report for? and 2) what is the report for?

Who is the report for?

You will get answer to this question by looking at your audience.
For the industry project, you have two distinct audiences: client and instructor.

Client may include senior management, customer, and general public.

Instructor falls under the domain of academic staff.

What is the report for?

The focus of your report could be the following:

  • Provide a background information
  • Make recommendation
  • Report on an experiment
  • Present data analysis

8.2 Writing Style

The writing styles can be classified into two: classic and practical.

Classic style of writing describes the world as one sees it. This style is followed by literary figures and mostly used in the work of fictions.

Practical style is language of formal documents such as research reports and memos where the writer and the reader have defined role. Thus, it follows a definite style (as outlined in the Chapter 6) and is succinct and precise.

For your final project report, it is advised that you follow the practical writing style.

Break the Rule

You can break away from the practical style by adopting classical style once in a while as it is direct, conversational, unfussy.

For example, practical style might say the sentence 'It is he' is grammatically correct, while 'It's him' is incorrect. Following the rule, you might kill the romance by saying 'Hi, Honey, it's I' or make the breakup ludicrous by saying 'It's not you, it's I'.

8.3 Useful Tips

Some of the good practices for writing your report are listed below:

Write in active voice. If required then use passive voice judiciously.

Develop a field specific vocabulary. Note that a specific word in a specific field have a very specific meaning. They are part of technical terminologies followed in the field. You should not abuse these terminologies by using them outside their unintended contexts. Best way to familiarize yourself with these technical terms is to read the literature thoroughly.

When you are describing a new idea or concept in a paragraph put it in the end. The paragraph should begin with familiar concepts on which you build upon your new idea. This helps readers in seeing the connections and makes it consistent. Use the rule Familiar first, Unfamiliar last.

When you argue something using numbers carefully describe what that number means (e.g., how it is operationalized, what is the unit etc.). Don’t just throw numbers at the readers.

Break your long sentences into smaller ones. You should try to put just one piece of information per sentence. Don’t use very long paragraphs. However, there should be logical transition from one paragraph to another.

Omit unnecessary words. Use simple words but be careful about the technical terms used in the field.

Sparingly use adjectives/adverbs.

Avoid repetitions of the same thing. Be concise.

Avoid abbreviations and acronyms.

Cross reference tables, figures, and sections by their number (and name) in the text.

Do spell-check and proofread carefully. Make sure the paragraphs are coherent and there is consistent flow in your texts.

Don’t use uninformative 'this' in the sentence, i.e., use of 'this' without clear antecedent. For example, "This shows that technology is important in a digital marketing environment". This what? This should always have something following it. For example,"This behavioral change shows that technology is important in a digital marketing environment".

Maintain clarity and consistency.

Use the normal sentence structure: subject, verb, object. Every sentence should have a subject, verb and object.

Put actions in verbs.

Put characters in subjects.

Keep subjects near verbs.

Tables are part of your writing. Titles and headings in tables should be self-explanatory. Use footnotes (or notes) in tables to make their readability clear. It helps skimming readers to understand the fact presented without having to go searching through the text.

Don’t overload your sentences.

Paragraphs should have structure and flow.

Use colon (:) and semicolon (;) properly. The semicolon (;) means roughly “furthermore” (or a parallel remark). The colon (:) means roughly “to be specific” (or an illustration to follow).

Don’t use commas (,), which aren’t necessary.

Rewrite (or revisit) every sentence you have written. For clarity, rearrange words, phrases, and paragraphs. Rewriting will help removing clutter.

Don’t succumb to the temptation of using business jargon.

Don’t start a sentence with a mathematical symbol.

Don’t say “which” when “that” sounds better. The general rule nowadays is to use “which” only when it is preceded by a comma or by a preposition, or when it is used interrogatively.

Use the correct number of significant digits, not whatever the program spits out. For example, stick with two or three significant digits.

Good and relevant figures make your report alive. They communicate the pattern in data visually, which is better than numbers in tables.

Use “we” instead of “I” in the report as it is an outcome of a group effort.

Don’t misuse hyphens (-). Hyphens are used for true compound modifiers before the noun (e.g., after-tax income, risk-free rate, two-day return). When there is no risk of misinterpretation, the hyphen can be omitted, but the treatment must be consistent throughout the paper.

“Where” refers to a place. “In which” refers to a model. Don’t write “models where consumers have uninsured shocks,” write “models in which consumers have uninsured shocks.”

Don’t overuse italics. It’s best to use them only when the emphasis in a sentence would otherwise not be clear.

8.4 Seinfeld’s Writing Rules

Jerry Seinfeld is the best comedian of our generation. How does he write all these jokes?

Technique that matters

You can’t do anything else. You don’t have to write, but you can’t do anything else.

No checking your phone. Either write or stare at a wall.

His creative fuel? A yellow legal pad with 20 pages of observations and half-baked ideas.

Stop the torture

You MUST have an end time for your writing session.

“If you have the guts to sit down and write, you need a reward at the end of the session.”

And that reward is the alarm goes off and you’re done..

Live for the re-write

95% of writing is re-write.

Jerry splits time across free play and polishing.

Free play is using the yellow legal pad to spur ideas.

Polishing is perfecting each word.

Making each joke ready for “the experiment”.

Run the experiment

You now have a bunch of words.

You then deliver them to an audience.

They dump a bunch of data on you.

“This is good, this is okay, this is terrible.”

Then you go back to re-writing and re-framing jokes or adding new ones.

Never break the chain

Jerry has a yearly calendar.

For each day he writes, he X’s that spot.

Over time, he builds a chain.

The longer the chain, more you have invested.

The more you have invested, the less you want to break it.

Treat yourself like a baby

“Writing is the most difficult thing in the world.”

So be kind to yourself.

“When writing, treat your brain like a toddler, and then when you look at it the next day, you want to be a hard-ass.”
